Twisting Tale of Young Sherlock Holmes

A new mystery unfolds in Oxford with danger, wit, scrolls, schemes, and the early chemistry between Sherlock Holmes and Moriarty. This feature sets the foggy, clever tone of the series.

Young Sherlock The Show

Set The Scene:

The blurry inside of a prison cell. Then we see a boy collecting bugs by a riverbank. His sister wants to play with him, but then she disappears. A man wakes up in a prison cell.

The Gist:

That man is Sherlock Holmes (Hero Fiennes Tiffin), who has been doing a six-month stint in prison for not just petty theft — he wanted to try out his pickpocketing skills but always gave people back what he takes — plus contempt of court. As he’s in the courtyard fighting a much bigger man, his older brother Mycroft (Max Irons) comes to bail him out.

After a haircut, shave and bath, Mycroft informs the college-age Sherlock that he’s gotten him into Oxford, and they’re going there so Mycroft can keep an eye on him. When they get to the university, though, Sherlock finds out that his brother didn’t enroll him, but got him a job as a porter, perhaps to keep him humble.

In the meantime, Gulun Shou’an (Zine Tseng), a young princess from China, is heading to Oxford to study, and when robbers intercept her carriage, looking for the ancient scrolls she has with her, they also kidnap her. But she can handle herself and returns to the carriage with the scrolls.

Sherlock studies and observes while cleaning up, taking particular interest in a math text written by Professor Charles Thompson. He catches the attention of both the princess and James Moriarty, beginning a dangerous and brilliant partnership.

“Well don’t mind me, I’m just an Ashkenazi side dish,” Moriarty says to the princess after she comes to see Sherlock after the scrolls are recovered.

Our Take:

While Young Sherlock certainly has the look of a Guy Ritchie production — modern soundtrack, tilting camera angles, lots of quippy action — the style doesn’t distract from the mystery. It still leaves room for Sherlock’s mind, his arrogance, his charm, and the tension of seeing Moriarty beside him before everything falls apart.

Production

Young Sherlock carries the signature Ritchie style we saw in The Gentlemen, but blends it with the logic games and mystery structure of classic Holmes adaptations.

The writers keep Sherlock recognizable: curious, reckless, observant, and brilliant. He still feels like the detective we know, just younger, less polished, and far more chaotic.

The series seems ready to balance episode mysteries with a larger murder arc, while slowly building the fracture between Sherlock and Moriarty.

Key Ideas of the Show
